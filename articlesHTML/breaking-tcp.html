Breaking Transmission Control Protocol

What is Transmission Control Protocol

Transmission Control Protocol, known as TCP, is a protocol developed sometime in the 70s. TCP is one part of the TCP/IP model, specifically at the transport layer. TCP was developed to allow computers to transfer data reliably and safely. This protocol accomplishes this by using a three-way handshake between the client and the host. Once this handshake is established they will exchange the data. TCP is very rigorous containing many safety checks including error detection and correction, sequencing, flow control, congestion control, and acknowledgments. So breaking TCP is not an easy task.

How could we "Break" TCP

As I stated earlier TCP is rigorous, but that does not mean it is foolproof. There a many ways that you could potentially go about bringing TCP to its knees. However, this is where I would like to split this section into two parts: artificial and natural. Both have their benefits, both have their negatives.

Artificial

It is no lie that people have made programs that can simulate poor network connections. Many Linux distributions are shipped with a command that can replicate these conditions from the get-go. The Linux tool "tc", known as Traffic Control, implements traffic delay and loss. This tool is very useful, but honestly, your delay is not going to stay at 10% all the time. Your delay will often vary depending on many different factors like propagation delay, transmission delay, processing delay, queuing delay, weather (if Wi-Fi), network topology, interference, etc. These factors are very hard to represent with a program as all of these issues often are random.

Natural

That brings us to the "natural" part of breaking TCP. As I stated at the end of the Artificial section, a lot of delay and loss can be random and not replicated through some programs. This is where you have to get creative. From the factors above we can try our best to make the worst conditions possible for our networking. Now, I cannot control the weather, but I can try to block the signals from reaching the router. One way I could do that is by using a Faraday shield, but I do not have access to one of those. So, I must try to find a corner in my house, or outside, where the wifi connection is not as strong. I can also strain the network with lots of data like YouTube, more devices, create bottlenecks, etc. I would like to try this strategy first as honestly it seems like more fun.
